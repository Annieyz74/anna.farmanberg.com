Efficiency Is the Outcome, Not the Starting Point

11 June 2026

AI agents are often presented as the next step in automation. They might be, but I believe we still talk too little about the other side. Agents create more work before they create less, and costs can escalate quickly if the fundamentals aren't in place.

I had this conversation recently with someone researching the topic. My advice was simple: start with the outcome. Then take an honest look at the investment required, and whether the efficiency gains will outweigh the cost.

AI isn't a magic wand. And this is something all major players, from Anthropic to OpenAI, have already highlighted. It takes thinking, preparation and real work to create the environment agents need to operate with quality. But in the day‑to‑day push for better automation, it's easy to forget.

The real cost structure behind AI agents

Here's a short checklist that helps outline the real cost structure behind AI agents. Of course, the details depend on each organization's starting point:

  1. Data quality and data capacity. Clean, structured, accessible, up to date data. It's an investment, not a side effect.
  2. Infrastructure and system capacity. Compute, API calls, storage, integrations. Agents are not "free power".
  3. Licensing and usage costs. Model licenses, agent licenses, user seats, API usage, token consumption. This is where CFOs start raising eyebrows.
  4. Governance and security. Agents need to be guided, monitored, logged and controlled. That's an organizational cost.
  5. Operations and maintenance. Agents are not "set and forget". They are systems, processes and workflows that require ongoing care and maintenance.
Efficiency is the outcome, not the starting point.

And AI agents are an investment, sometimes a significant one depending on mission and purpose. The value appears when the cost is lower than the efficiency gained.

Something worth keeping in mind as the agent conversation accelerates: the cost increases exponentially with complexity and volume.
