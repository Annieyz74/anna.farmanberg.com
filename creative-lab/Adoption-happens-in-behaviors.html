Adoption Happens in Behaviors, Not Dashboards

5 May 2026

Adoption happens in behaviors, not dashboards. Yet it's often treated as KPIs and usage rates. Whether you call it adoption, implementation, activation or customer migration, the principle is the same:

Adoption isn't about the switch from "not using" to "using." It's about how people learn, adapt — and the support they need to take the next step. Adoption always starts with people.

Three dimensions of adoption

Many of us know Everett Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations — the classic adoption curve that explains when adoption happens in a group: Innovators → Early Adopters → Early Majority → Late Majority → Laggards.

But timing alone won't drive real adoption. We need to look at behaviors to understand how people adopt. I believe in combining three dimensions:

  • ·Rogers' adoption curve — when people adopt
  • ·Adoption work profiles — how people respond to change
  • ·Learning & adoption styles — what people need to move forward

Adoption work profiles

A practical way to segment how people actually respond to change:

  • ·Champions — drive adoption, influence others, try new things early
  • ·Supporters — positive and willing, but don't drive on their own
  • ·Neutrals — need clarity and "what's in it for me"
  • ·Skeptics — question and require proof
  • ·Resisters — actively or passively push back

Learning & adoption styles

  • ·Self-learners — explore on their own
  • ·Guided learners — need onboarding, structure, step-by-step
  • ·Social learners — learn through others
  • ·Skeptical learners — need to understand the "why" before investing time

Why this matters

A person can be a Champion with low usage — motivated but unsure how → training helps. Or a Skeptic with high usage — uses the tool but doubts the value → show impact. Or a Neutral with passive usage — a "sleeping giant" where the right trigger unlocks a lot.

When we bring these three dimensions together, adoption becomes more meaningful, more human — and far more successful.

Think about your own team or customers — how do people learn and adapt when something new arrives?
