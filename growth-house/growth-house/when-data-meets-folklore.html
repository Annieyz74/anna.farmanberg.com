When Data Meets Folklore — The Missing Piece in Your AI Strategy

14 April 2026

Imagine what AI could do if its real power came from combining data with organizational folklore — the quiet everyday knowledge spread across people, conversations and old systems. The knowledge that actually holds the work together.

The gap between data and relevance

Last week I wrote about folklore as a cultural phenomenon. This week is about organizational folklore. McKinsey calls it folklore data and Anthropic describes it as the gap between data and relevance.

My experience is the same. You look at dashboards or spreadsheets, try to add complementary information to turn data into insight — and suddenly it's obvious that the CRM view is incomplete. That's where you see that it's rarely the technology that slows things down, but the missing folklore and context.

AI can analyze and automate. But it cannot interpret culture, relationships or judgment.

That's why I believe many organizations need to zoom out for a moment and pause — to look at their own reality before they keep running.

Understanding your own folklore

It starts with asking the right questions:

  • ·What do we know?
  • ·What do we need to know?
  • ·Where is the knowledge?
  • ·Where do we miss it?
  • ·What lives in people's heads, notebooks, Slack threads and old systems?

Then comes the work of gathering and surfacing it. It doesn't need to be perfect. It doesn't need to be complete. It just needs to create context.

Start small and iterate

Many companies try to run ahead of their own reality when working with AI. They want automation and integration — but to succeed, they first need to understand their own folklore.

Because once we see it, we can gather it. And once we've gathered it, we can integrate it with data. That's when AI becomes truly meaningful: when data and folklore finally meet.

Dare to start. Begin with what you have. Take one step. Learn as you go and adjust along the way.

How do you start uncovering the folklore in your own organization?
