The Day I Understood What a Personal Brand Really Is

26 March 2026

My father gave me a piece of advice when I was 16. I didn't fully understand it then.

I had just landed my first summer job — excited, nervous, and very convinced that I already knew most things worth knowing. His advice was simple:

Treat everyone with respect, the way you want to be treated yourself.

You don't have to love everyone, but respect matters. It's a small world. You never know when your paths will cross again. That person could become your future manager, colleague, customer, neighbor or supplier.

I smiled politely. And quietly pretended I understood him.

The moment it clicked

A few years later, working at a niched tour operator, something wasn't going the way I needed it to. I was trying — politely but firmly — to move things forward. Without much success.

The person in front of me paused, looked at my business card and said thoughtfully: "That's quite an unusual surname."

He mentioned my father's name. They had worked together years ago, before I was even born. He handed over his passport, smiled, and said he knew it was in safe hands.

That was the moment I truly understood what a personal brand is. And why it matters.

What a brand actually is

Your brand isn't your title. It isn't your logo. It isn't your LinkedIn headline.

It's how people feel when they hear your name.

My father had built his — quietly, consistently, with respect — long before I ever needed it. Companies do the same. The strongest brands aren't built in campaigns. They're built in every meeting, every email, every interaction, long before anyone is watching.

Think about a brand you trust. Chances are, it's because of a person — not a campaign.

You already have a personal brand. Everyone does. The question is — are you building it intentionally?
