Three Ways of Fishing — And Three Ways of Working

23 June 2026

I learned three ways of fishing as a kid, and I still use all three in my work today.

Sweden has a long coastline, thousands of lakes and rivers — chances are you've gone fishing at some point.

Before the internet and mobile phones (and back when the ones that existed were anything but mobile), I could spend hours on the jetty. Sometimes fishing, sometimes catching sticklebacks with a small hand net. Some of my happiest childhood memories come from those summer breaks in Stockholm's archipelago — sitting on a small jetty or in a tiny boat, staring at the float and hoping for the catch of the day. It was simple, joyful, peaceful, and somehow grounding.

When I was around seven, my father gave me an old casting rod. It turned out he had received it from his father when he was a boy, and now he had fixed it up for me. I was beaming with delight — this was going to level up my fishing experience, I thought.

I started practicing the technique on land first, using a practice spinner. It sure required more precision, more attention, and was definitely an upgrade from my old rod. Soon I proudly moved on to casting from the jetty.

But the most exciting moments were when we used the fishing nets. We'd lay them out in the evening, after sunset, when the water was calm and the archipelago quiet. Early the next morning we'd go down to see if we had any luck. We usually did — quite a lot of perch and other fish.

Years passed and the seal population grew. And eventually, there were no fish left in that particular strait.

That's when I realized something: different fishing techniques require different skills and different conditions.

Three methods, three ways of working

🎣 Float fishing — The quiet method. Listening, presence, patience. Letting things take time when the conditions are right. In work, this is relationships, trust, and insight. The slow things that build the important things.

🎣 Casting rod — Curiosity and throwing further. Finding the places others haven't explored yet. Testing, exploring, following your own direction first. This is where I often end up, and it's a strength, when you know it's the right method.

🎣 Nets — Structure, systems, and the bigger picture. When you need to catch more than one thing at a time. When you build processes, not just ideas.

And then… the seals

In the archipelago, they eat the fish and change the conditions. They force you further out into open water. In work, the seals are everything that eats opportunities:

  • ·Unclear priorities
  • ·Internal politics — when it stops ideas instead of shaping them
  • ·Too many directions
  • ·Too little mandate
  • ·Too much noise

It's not that you lack patience. It's that the water lacks fish.

The point

We need all three methods: float fishing, casting and nets. And sometimes we need to accept that the seals have taken over the strait — that it's time to change location, change method, or go further out.

Perhaps that's the real lesson: knowing when to wait, when to cast, and when to move on.
